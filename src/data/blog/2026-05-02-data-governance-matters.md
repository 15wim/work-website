# Why Data Governance Actually Matters

Data governance has a reputation problem.

Mention it in a boardroom and you get compliance slides. Mention it to a data team and you get eye rolls. Somewhere between the policy documents and the data catalogs, the actual point gets lost — which is this: **governance is how an organization earns the right to trust its own data.**

## What Most Organizations Get Wrong

The most common mistake I see is treating governance as a documentation exercise. You create a data dictionary. You assign data stewards. You run a training session. You declare success.

Then six months later, two teams are reporting different revenue numbers to the same executive and nobody knows which one is right.

The problem is that governance was treated as a deliverable, not an operating model. Documents do not govern data. People do — when they understand what they own, why it matters, and what happens when quality breaks down.

## What Actually Works

At Innovation FCU, we approached Microsoft Purview implementation not as a cataloging project, but as an organizational accountability exercise. Every data domain needed a named owner who understood the downstream consumers of their data. Every dataset that fed a regulatory report needed traceable lineage. Every quality failure needed a path to resolution that did not require a VP to intervene.

The catalog was the artifact. The operating model was the point.

Three things that changed outcomes:

**1. Ownership before tooling.** We defined data stewards and their responsibilities before we turned on Purview. The tool reflects ownership clarity — it does not create it.

**2. Business-facing language.** Most governance frameworks describe data in technical terms. Our documentation described what the data is used for, who consumes it, and what breaks when it is wrong. Business stakeholders could actually use it.

**3. Governance connected to real risk.** When a data quality issue in our RESL portfolio logic could affect a regulatory submission, everyone paid attention. Abstract governance principles compete with delivery pressure. Concrete risk consequences do not.

## The Long Game

Governance done well becomes invisible. You stop having debates about which number is right. You stop spending analyst hours on data reconciliation. You start spending that capacity on actual insight.

That is the payoff. Not the catalog. Not the lineage diagram. The hours your team gets back to do the work that actually moves the business.

I am still learning what works and what does not. But after leading this at two institutions now, I am confident of one thing: **the organizations that treat governance as an operating discipline — not a compliance project — are the ones that become genuinely data-driven.**

The rest are still reconciling their revenue numbers.
